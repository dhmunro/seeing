Two stories of gravity

Isaac Newton invented physics with his theory of universal gravitation to explain planetary motion: Every pair of bodies attract each another by a force directly proportional to both masses, and inversely proportional to the distance between them, F = GMm/r². As you will see, he arrived at this rule by applying his ideas about force and motion to the orbits of planets and moons in the solar system. This combination of abstract theory - three mathematical laws of motion - with experimental observations - Kepler's description of planetary motion - has served as the archetype for physical science ever since.

The impact of Newton's work on the way we think about and work with nature cannot be overstated. The excitement over understanding celestial mechanics very quickly spread throughout the scientific and engineering world as people began to apply Newton's mathematical modeling approach to countless different problems. Newtonian mechanics ignited, then fueled the industrial revolution.

Shockingly, two centuries after Newton, Albert Einstein invented a completely different way to picture gravity, also based on general laws of mechanics, but updated to be consistent with new experiments involving the speed of light. Like the step Newton took beyond Kepler, Einstein's new picture is carefully constructed to be equivalent to Newton's everywhere it had been tested up to that time. Deviations begin only in cases involving bodies moving near the speed of light - thousands of times faster than anything in the solar system. Therefore, unlike Newton, even today very few physicists - let alone engineers - ever use Einstein's theory of gravity. Einstein's math is far more complicated, and errors in Newton's theory are usually negligible.

You will not find any details about Einstein's theory of gravity (a.k.a. general relativity) here. However, you will learn how Newton's first law of motion defines the concept of a straight line in space-time - the four dimensional universe we all inhabit. Once you know a straight line, you can recognize a curved one, and thus glimpse gravity from Einstein's perspective of curved space-time.

Newton's laws of motion and gravity

  1. A body at rest stays at rest, and a body in motion stays in motion in a straight line at a constant speed, unless acted on by a force.
  2. The force on a body is directly proportional to, and in the same direction as, its acceleration. The constant of proportionality is the mass of the body.
  3. To every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. That is, the forces of two bodies on each other are always equal and in opposite directions.

The first law is, in a sense, a special case of the second - namely the case of zero force. However, the first law really defines what "straight line" means in space-time according to Einstein, as you'll see later. The second law is the iconic F = ma, which defines both force and mass in a deliberately circular manner. The third law is what physicists now call conservation of momentum; it limits how bodies can interact.

Newton applied these laws to the case of the planets: At a given instant of time, you know from Kepler's laws that the Earth is accelerating directly toward the Sun at at a rate inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, say a = K/r². Furthermore, things accelerate directly toward the Earth according to a different inverse square law, say g = k/r², where the Earth's constant of proportionality k is smaller than the Sun's K by a factor of about 330,000. The second law of motion asserts that the force of the Sun pulling on Earth is proportional to the mass m of the Earth, F = ma, and also that the force of the Earth pulling on the Sun is proportional to the mass M of the Sun, F = Mg.

Enter the third law: The force of the Sun pulling on the Earth must equal the force of the Earth pulling on the Sun, F = ma = Mg. (They act in opposite directions along the line connecting them.) Newton's law of equal action and reaction plus the experimental inverse square acceleration laws for the Sun and Earth require that the observed ratio of acceleration constants K/k must equal the ratio of masses M/m (and the ratio of accelerations a/g). Thus, the Sun must be 330,000 times more massive that the Earth.

Furthermore, the ratio K/M must equal k/m. Call this ratio G. You could have made this argument with Jupiter or any other planet instead of Earth, so the ratio G = K/M must be the same for every body. In other words, any two bodies attract each other with a force proportional to both masses, F = GMm/r², where G is a universal constant of nature - the first such constant ever discovered. Since the acceleration constants K = GM and k = Gm, this universal law of gravitational force identifies the mass of any object as the source of the gravitational acceleration of all other objects toward it.

You have learned something completely new by applying the second and third laws of motion to the planets: Mass is the source of gravity! Newton's reasoning is quite simple, but turns out to be very deep. (The technically difficult part of universal gravitation is figuring out that Kepler's laws are equivalent to an inverse square law of acceleration - see Touching the Solar System.) The next step is to try to explain how the inverse square law of gravitational attraction operates in a geometrical way.

Gravitational field lines

Gravity is an acceleration vector at every point in space - the gravitational acceleration a small mass would experience at that point. According to universal gravitation, to compute this, you have to add the acceleration vectors GM/r² directed toward every other particle of mass M and distance r in the universe. Start by considering the acceleration produced by just a single idealized point mass - a particle so much smaller than its distance r that you can ignore its dimensions. The planets are good examples of point masses, since they appear as mere pinpoints of light from anywhere else in the solar system.

A vector associated with every point in space is called a vector field. Michael Faraday invented a way to visualize vector fields about a century after Newton's death. Faraday pictured lines of force filling space - a force field. He was looking at the lines traced by iron filings on a sheet of paper with a magnet beneath, but the picture works for gravity as well. The idea is to connect the vectors into (possibly curved) lines - the path you would follow if you kept moving in the direction the vectors point. So you picture the gravity of a point mass as myriad radial rays converging to the point, like long pins in a tiny pincushion. Each ray is called a field line. Notice that a field line is a directed line - like a one-way street. This field picture captures the direction of the acceleration vector everywhere, but at first glance it seems to lose any indication of magnitude - the length of the arrow.

However, the spacing between the lines decreases as they converge to the point mass. You can define their density at a given position along some field line by imagining a small loop around the field line. The loop will encircle other nearby lines, and you can define the density of field lines at that location to be the number of field lines threading through this tiny loop, divided by the area of the loop. If you move farther away from the point mass, you need to scale up the diameter of the loop in proportion to the distance for it to encircle a fixed set of field lines, and the loop area scales as the square of the diameter - hence distance from the point mass. Thus, the number of field lines per unit area scales inversely as the square of the distance! If you make the total number of lines emanating from the point proportional to its mass, the magnitude of the gravitational acceleration will be proportional to the number of field lines per unit area!

So far, you assumed the plane of the loop was perpendicular to the field lines. What happens if the plane of the loop is tilted relative to the field lines? The number of lines threading the loop will be proportional to the area of the projection of the loop into the plane perpendicular to the field line. The ratio of that projected area to the loop area when it was oriented normally is exactly the same as the ratio of the component of the acceleration normal to the tilted loop to the full acceleration, which is the component along the field line. Therefore, for any small loop, the component of acceleration normal to the plane of the loop is the number of field lines threading the loop per unit area. More subtly, you need to choose the sign of the component to be negative if the directed field lines thread the loop in the direction opposite to its normal.

Another view of the inverse square law

Imagine a closed surface of any size (the surface of the Earth will soon become an example). A point mass at some arbitrary position produces an acceleration which varies as you move around to different points on the surface. The component of that acceleration normal to the surface (here the inward normal) thus also varies around the surface. The field picture makes it easy to compute the area-weighted average of this normal component of acceleration over the whole surface:

Since the normal acceleration component is the number of lines per unit area threading any small loop on the surface, its area-weighted average is just the total number of field lines piercing the surface divided by the total surface area! The one caveat is that you have to count each inward piercing line as plus one, and each outward piercing line as minus one, in order to be consistent about the sign of the acceleration relative to the surface normal direction you have selected.

There are two cases: First, if the point mass is outside the surface, then each field line pierces the surface twice - once going inward and a second time going outward, so the net number of piercings is zero. Second, if the point mass is inside the surface, then each field line pierces just once, on its way inward. (If the surface is not convex, there may be additional pairs of inward and outward piercings for some rays, but such pairs cancel each other.) Thus, if the point mass is outside the surface, the mean value of the normal component of gravitational acceleration over the whole surface is always zero. When the point mass is inside the surface, on the other hand, the mean value of the normal acceleration is always the total number of field lines converging on the point mass, divided by the total surface area.

A second point mass adds its acceleration vector to the acceleration from the first at every point in space. Because the acceleration vectors simply add, the averages of the inward component of the acceleration also simply add. Thus, if the new point is inside the surface, the average increases by the new mass divided by the surface area, otherwise the new mass contributes nothing. Accumulating more point masses in this way, you find that the inverse square law of universal gravitation has this remarkable property:

The average value of the inward acceleration component over any closed surface is proportional the the total mass inside that surface divided by the surface area.

The constant of proportionality is 4𝝅G, as you can work out from a spherical mass distribution. This is a major result of vector calculus, called Gauss's law, discovered about a century after Newton. Faraday's powerful field line picture has made it almost obvious, showing you its geometrical significance - total number of field line piercings over total surface area. It is equivalent to the inverse square law, with the advantage that instead of "the square of the distance" you can think about the area of a surface.

Testing the theory

Newton faced an immediate technical problem with his theory of universal gravitation: In the argument that the Earth's gravity diminishes as the square of distance between your downward acceleration on its surface and the acceleration of the Moon in its orbit, you assumed that your "distance from the Earth" was the radius of the Earth, that is, your distance from the center of the Earth. But with universal gravitation, the idea is that each little piece of the Earth - every rock and grain of sand and drop of magma in the whole ball - is individually pulling on you. When you add up all those individual forces in different directions, why should you get the same answer as if all that mass were concentrated at the center of the Earth? Unlike Newton, you can use Gauss's law to answer this question.

For any spherically symmetric object, the acceleration at its surface must point directly inward toward the center, and have the same value at every point of the surface - which is therefore the average inward component of the acceleration. Thus, the gravitational acceleration at the surface depends only on the total mass of the object divided by the surface area. How the mass is distributed does not matter, as long as it is spherically symmetric (otherwise the acceleration would differ from place to place around the surface). In particular, the acceleration at the surface is the same as if all the mass were condensed to a single point at its center. You have resolved Newton's technical problem, and universal gravitation has passed an important consistency test. (And you can work out the 4𝝅G constant mentioned above!)

Kepler's laws are equivalent to Newton's inverse square gravitation only when there are just two bodies, a smaller one orbiting a very much larger one. The motion of the planets is dominated by the Sun; the motion of the moons by their planet. However, according to universal gravitation, all the planets and moons are attracting one another.

In fact, our Moon is accelerating toward the Earth at about 1/3600 of the "one gee" surface gravity you feel, while it is accelerating toward the Sun over twice as fast, about 1/1650 gee. Although the Earth accelerates toward the Sun at nearly the same rate, their distances from the Sun differ by up to one part in 400, so according to the inverse square law, the accelerations differ by up one part in 200. Newton himself spent years calculating the details of how this time varying half percent difference perturbs the orbit of the Moon, showing that the gravity of the Sun accounts for all the irregularities of the Moon's orbit known since ancient times.

The idea that every mass attracts every other according to the inverse square law similarly explains a great many small deviations from Kepler's laws in the solar system as the result of attractions between multiple bodies. In fact, unexplained perturbations in the orbit of Uranus led astronomers to predict and then discover Neptune.

Universal gravitation has immense built-in complexity because every pair of bodies attract each other, while at the same time a great simplicity in the rule of attraction between each pair. Complexity is a crucial feature, inasmuch as the motions in the solar system really do become more and more intricate the closer you look. Far from being completely deterministic, as people long assumed Newtonian mechanics to be, the fate of the solar system is provably unknowable on timescales of a hundred million years (called its Lyapunov time).

Inverse square law, take three

You already have two different ways to think about the inverse square law: Either each particle of matter causes everything else to accelerate toward it in proportion to its mass, or the area-averaged inward acceleration over any closed surface is proportional to the total mass inside the surface. Just as you might want to view a scene from as many perspectives as possible, different ways to describe gravity are very helpful.

Gravitational potential provides a third important view of gravity. The acceleration field, central to your first two pictures, is rather complicated - you have to imagine a vector arrow at each point in space. The idea of potential is to reduce this vector to a single number at each point. Of course, you still need to be able to reconstruct the acceleration from these numbers; one way to do that is to make the component of the vector in any direction be proportional to the rate of change of the numbers in that direction; in other words, the vector is the gradient. Toward space-time geometry

Motion occupies four dimensions: Three spatial coordinates for position in space, plus one time coordinate. Of course, the time dimension is quite different from the other three, so it is not at all obvious how to combine space and time into anything like geometry. However, the motion of solid bodies, or even of the tiniest particles of matter, stitches space and time together. Although the analogy with Euclidean lines in three dimensional space is not complete, a particle of matter does trace a path through space-time called its world-line. Objects collide if and only if their world-lines intersect in space-time, and the position and time of such a collision is called an event - the analog of a Euclidean point.

Newton thought of space-time as slices of constant time stacked like cards in a deck, with the positions of every particle of matter marked by a dot (an event!) on each card. While everyone agrees on what every card looks like, and on the time interval between cards (the thickness of a card), you don't need to square the deck the way it comes out of the box. You can shear or fan the deck any way you please (as long as you don't change the order of the cards by shuffling). No matter how you shear the deck, the world-lines of all the particles connect the cards together - you don't have any choice about how to draw world-lines. You can't reorder the deck, since each card is a snapshot in time, and you can only smoothly shear or twist the cards - any discontinuous jump would break the world lines into two parts. Motion of material particles is smooth.

Typically, you decide to stack the deck of constant time slices in order to make one particular body be "at rest". That is, you tie the origin of your spatial coordinate system to some material object. For example, ancient astronomers chose the Earth to be fixed in space, while Copernicus (and Kepler) chose the Sun. You can argue about which choice makes the motion of the planets (the problem at hand for astronomers) simpler - and indeed what constitutes simplicity - but in the end, how you choose to stack the deck is up to you. In fact, if you allow the orientation of each spatial slice to change, so that your spatial coordinate systems can rotate, then you have even more choices, corresponding to fanning the deck in addition to shearing it. (For example, you may want to regard your observatory as motionless, in which case your coordinate system will rotate with the Earth.)

But Newton's laws of motion tell you how bodies move, that is, the shapes of their world-lines in space-time. According to the first law, a body with no net force acting on it moves at a constant speed (possibly zero) in a straight line. This law very strongly constrains how you are allowed to stack your slices of constant time: You have to stack the deck in such a way that bodies with no net forces acting on them follow straight world-lines through space-time - otherwise Newton's first law of motion won't be obeyed! A stacking which preserves Newton's first law is called an inertial frame of reference, as distinguished from an accelerated frame of reference.

From his stack-of-cards perspective of space-time, Newton interpreted his first law to mean that you should stack time slices to make the world-lines of small bodies at a great distance from any masses with significant gravity be straight lines (through space-time as well as through ordinary space). The bodies comprising the solar system then inherit this stacking from the hypothetical very distant non-gravitating bodies. Judged by such an "inertial at infinity" stacking, the velocities of planets and moons clearly accelerate, and therefore, according to the first law, must have a net force - the force of gravity - acting upon them. Planets and moons then follow curved - accelerated - paths through space-time, as well as through ordinary space.

Curved space-time

A non-inertial reference frame often offers the simplest framework for talking about a phenomenon. For example, you usually choose the ground to be fixed when you discuss the motion of projectiles near Earth's surface, despite the fact that the ground rotates with the Earth, which rotates around the Sun - the ground is not an inertial frame! When you do choose a rotating or otherwise accelerated reference frame, you must take into account forces, such as the centrifugal force and the Coriolis force, which depend on the acceleration of your reference frame, and which would not be present had you chosen an inertial frame.

These frame dependent forces share a very striking feature of gravitational force: The force on a body is always proportional to its mass - always producing the same acceleration independent of mass. The reason is this common acceleration (or its negative) really belongs the reference frame. Einstein proposed that what Newton called gravitational force was of exactly the same character as, say, centrifugal force - namely that Newton has chosen to work in a non-inertial reference frame.

However, in order for Einstein's proposal to make any sense, he has to change the rules Newton played by: Newton only permitted you to make a single choice of reference frame - once you choose how to stack them, you have to stick with your choice once and for all. Einstein dispensed with this one-choice rule, allowing you to change the way you stack the time slices as often as you please, choosing your stacking to apply for only a limited region of space and time around the current position of the bodies of interest.

If you allow your space-time model this flexibility, you can indeed eliminate all gravitational forces, instead ascribing the gravitational acceleration to your local inertial frame relative to Newton's global inertial frame. In other words, Einstein permits gravity to locally bend the way you stack time-slices; gravity curves space-time. (As long as nothing moves near lightspeed, the slices of constant time are always ordinary flat three dimensional space - the curvature is entirely in the stacking of these slices.)

To be clear, Einstein is not saying that Newton's reference frame is wrong, merely that it is a choice. If you do choose to work in Newton's frame (as nearly everyone still does today), then you interpret the motion of bodies to be influenced by gravitational forces, exactly as Newton did. What Einstein is saying is that instead of taking this view, you can instead imagine that any acceleration you observe belongs to a local inertial frame of reference, which is falling along with the object. In other words, a body with no forces being applied to it, like an orbiting spacecraft, drags along its own inertial reference frame in which it is always at rest. It is not the body that is falling around its orbit, but this constantly changing reference frame.

What Newton calls an inertial frame is infinite in extent both in space and time, and only bodies infinitely far from any gravitating mass have motions satisfying his first law. With such a reference frame, you need to account for gravitational forces and their accelerations. Einstein, on the other hand, allows you to choose local inertial reference frames, like the one moving along with a spacecraft, which are limited in extent both in space and time. In those local frames, there are no gravitational forces or accelerations. Instead, you need to slowly update the acceleration of your local inertial frame relative to Newton's global inertial frame. What Newton calls gravitational acceleration, Einstein permits you to call (negative) acceleration of your local inertial frame.

Even Newton lamented the action-at-a-distance feature of his inverse square law. Einstein is pointing out that this is really a feature of the global reference frame Newton is choosing. If you use only local inertial frames, there is no longer a problem with action-at-a-distance, because you can dispense with the inverse square force law entirely. But to do this, Einstein has paid a very heavy price - to track planetary orbits, he must patch together a huge number of tiny local inertial frames, following the planet as it moves around the Sun. Newton's gravitational field he re-interprets as the (negative) acceleration of each of these reference frame patches, rather than as an acceleration caused by any gravitational force. In other words, the gravitational field tells you the acceleration of a local inertial frame (relative to Newton's global frame), and the planet always travels in a straight line through space-time in its current local reference frame.

Once you have defined a straight world-line, you can, of course, recognize a curved world-line. Newton's second law of motion supplies a measure of the curvature of the world-line of a body: its acceleration. That is, acceleration is what you mean by the curvature of space-time - how much world-lines deviate from being straight. The rubber sheets you sometimes see to illustrate Einstein's ideas about space-time curvature are terribly misleading. Curved space-time has nothing to do with the three dimensions of space being warped, at least for low speed motions like the planets in the solar system. Three dimensional space in the solar system is perfectly flat and Euclidean - the curvature is all in the time direction. Acceleration is the space-time analog of curvature.

These are all statements of experimental fact - a distillation of decades, if not millenia, of careful observations of the sky. This massive data reduction required great skill in mathematics, ranging from geometry and arithmetic through calculus - and every single one of those skills was developed, at least in part, to work specifically on the problem of planetary motion.

Separately, Newton formulated three general laws of motion by thinking about the mechanics of earthbound objects. His theory of gravity emerges when you interpret the experimental laws of planetary motion according to these general laws of mechanics. This bold idea - essentially that the Earth and heavenly bodies are just very large rocks - follows directly from the experimental fact that apples and the Moon follow the inverse square acceleration law. In other words, you already know that the gravity you feel on Earth is what holds the Moon in its orbit. What if the pull of Earth extends all the way to the Sun? Indeed, what if the Sun and Jupiter extert their own gravitational pull to hold their planets and moons in orbit? What if every particle of matter pulls on every other?

Isaac Newton, the great mathematician, translated Kepler's laws of planetary motion into an inverse square law of acceleration. From the fact that the Moon, 60 times farther from Earth's center than we are, moves with a downward acceleration 3600 (60²) times less than objects we drop on Earth's surface, Newton the great physicist deduced that the gravity we all feel at the surface of the Earth is exactly what keeps the Moon circling the Earth. Gravity not holds us down, it is binds the whole solar system together.

The reason Newton was thinking about acceleration in the first place was to apply his general theory of mechanics - how things move - to the planets. When he did, he discovered a universal law of gravity. Universal gravitation explains why the Moon does not accurately orbit according to Kepler's laws, how the Moon and Sun produce ocean tides, the tiny deviations in in planetary orbits from Kepler's laws, and many other puzzles.

Here you will follow Newton's physical reasoning about mechanics and planetary motion. Although Newton's model of gravity remains the easiest way to think about both solar system dynamics and motion in general, and despite its overwhelming success in describing a wide variety of phenomena, Einstein pointed out a different way to picture gravity. Just as Newton's model of gravity began as a different, but equivalent, way to think about Kepler's laws of planetary motion, so Einstein's model of gravity begins as a different, but equivalent, way to think about Newton's inverse square force law. All three pictures remain relevant today - astronomers choose the viewpoint best suited to describe the problem at hand.

Mass and weight

The translated Kepler's laws state that any bodies at the same distance from an attracting center will share a common acceleration, no matter their size: A cannonball and a bullet accelerate downward at the same rate, and a comet is accelerating toward the Sun at the same rate as Earth when it crosses Earth's orbit. That means the gravitational force must be directly proportional to the mass of the falling body, according to Newton's second law of motion.

However, if gravity pulls you toward the Earth, then Newton's third law says you must pull up on the Earth with an equal force - the force down on you is the action, and the force up on the Earth is the equal and opposite reaction. Similarly, if the gravity of the Sun holds the Earth in orbit, the Earth must pull back with the same force on the Sun. The second law told you the Sun's gravitational force on the Earth is proportional to Earth's mass, since you know that any sized object at the same distance would share the same acceleration. Equal and opposite reaction

Newton knew from Kepler that the planets accelerate directly toward the Sun at a rate inversely proportional to the square of their distance. Thus, since F = ma, the Sun must pull on the planet with a force proportional to the mass of the planet (and inversely proportional to the square of the distance). But you also know that each planet (or both Jupiter and Earth, at least) causes things to accelerate toward it according to an inverse square distance law. If this plantary inverse square law extends all the way to the Sun, then the planet must be causing the Sun to accelerate (very slightly) toward it as well - which means the planet pulls on the Sun with a force proportional to the solar mass.

But the force of the Sun pulling on the planet must equal the force of the planet pulling on the Sun, since actions must have equal and opposite reactions. (The forces are clearly in opposite directions along the Sun-planet line.) Since the pull of the Sun is proportional to the planet's mass and the pull of the planet proportional to the Sun's mass, the Sun-planet force must be directly proportional to both masses, as well as inversely proportional to the square of the distance. From this, Newton proposed his law of universal gravitation - that a force acts between any pair of bodies directly proportional to both masses, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, F = GMm/r².

Notice that the inverse square law acceleration constant for a body of mass M is GM, directly proportional to its mass. Thus, Newton deduced that the Sun is roughly a thousand times the mass of Jupiter, while Jupiter is a bit over three hundred times the mass of Earth, since those are the ratios of their acceleration constants from Kepler's laws. This is still the only way we know the masses of stars, planets, asteroids, or black holes - by measuring the accelerations of nearby objects.

On the one hand, universal gravitation represents a huge simplification - a single inverse square force law explains all manner of phenomena, from the motion of the planets or cannonballs to the tides. On the other hand, you realize that in order to compute the motion of any object, you need to account for the forces between every rock and drop of fluid in the whole universe! Every pair of rocks attract each other, so actually applying the "simple" law can become very complicated indeed.
