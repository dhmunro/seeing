Two stories of gravity

Isaac Newton invented physics with his theory of universal gravitation to explain planetary motion: Every pair of bodies attract each another by a force directly proportional to both masses, and inversely proportional to the distance between them, F = GMm/r². As you will see, he arrived at this rule by applying his ideas about force and motion to the orbits of planets and moons in the solar system. This combination of abstract theory - three mathematical laws of motion - with experimental observations - Kepler's description of planetary motion - has served as the archetype for physical science ever since.

The impact of Newton's work on the way we think about and work with nature cannot be overstated. The excitement over understanding celestial mechanics very quickly spread throughout the scientific and engineering world as people began to apply Newton's mathematical modeling approach to countless different problems. Newtonian mechanics ignited, then fueled the industrial revolution.

Shockingly, two centuries after Newton, Albert Einstein invented a completely different way to picture gravity, also based on general laws of mechanics, but updated to be consistent with new experiments involving the speed of light. Like the step Newton took beyond Kepler, Einstein's new picture is carefully constructed to be equivalent to Newton's everywhere it had been tested up to that time. Deviations begin only in cases involving bodies moving near the speed of light - thousands of times faster than anything in the solar system. Therefore, unlike Newton, even today very few physicists - let alone engineers - ever use Einstein's theory of gravity. Einstein's math is far more complicated, and errors in Newton's theory are usually negligible.

You will not find any details about Einstein's theory of gravity (a.k.a. general relativity) here. However, you will learn how Newton's first law of motion defines the concept of a straight line in space-time - the four dimensional universe we all inhabit. Once you know a straight line, you can recognize a curved one, and thus glimpse gravity from Einstein's perspective of curved space-time. Even Newton disliked the action-at-a-distance feature of his universal gravitation - the fact that the force law connects things across great distances of empty space. In contrast, Einstein's geometrical picture is local from the beginning.

Newton's laws of motion and gravity

  1. A body at rest stays at rest, and a body in motion stays in motion in a straight line at a constant speed, unless acted on by a force.
  2. The force on a body is directly proportional to, and in the same direction as, its acceleration. The constant of proportionality is the mass of the body.
  3. To every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. That is, the forces of two bodies on each other are always equal and in opposite directions.

The first law is, in a sense, a special case of the second - namely the case of zero force. However, the first law really defines what "straight line" means in space-time according to Einstein, as you'll see later. The second law is the iconic F = ma, which defines both force and mass in a deliberately circular manner. The third law is what physicists now call conservation of momentum.

When he applied these general laws of motion to the case of planets, Newton learned something completely new: mass is the source of gravity. His reasoning is quite simple, but turns out to be very deep. (The technically difficult part of universal gravitation is figuring out that Kepler's laws are equivalent to an inverse square law of acceleration - see Touching the Solar System.)

At a given instant of time, you know from Kepler's laws that the Earth is accelerating directly toward the Sun at at a rate inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, say a = K/r². Furthermore, things accelerate directly toward the Earth according to a different inverse square law, say g = k/r², where the Earth's constant of proportionality k is smaller than the Sun's K by a factor of about 330,000. The second law of motion asserts that the force of the Sun pulling on Earth is proportional to the mass m of the Earth, F = ma, and also that the force of the Earth pulling on the Sun is proportional to the mass M of the Sun, F = Mg.

Enter the third law: The force of the Sun pulling on the Earth must equal the force of the Earth pulling on the Sun, F = ma = Mg. (They act in opposite directions along the line connecting them.) Newton's law of equal action and reaction plus the experimental inverse square acceleration laws for the Sun and Earth require that the observed ratio of acceleration constants K/k must equal the ratio of masses M/m (and the ratio of accelerations a/g). Thus, the Sun must be 330,000 times more massive that the Earth.

Furthermore, the ratio K/M must equal k/m. Newton called this ratio G. You could have made this argument with Jupiter or any other planet instead of Earth, so the ratio G = K/M must be the same for every body. In other words, any two bodies attract each other with a force proportional to both masses, F = GMm/r², where G is a universal constant of nature - the first such constant ever discovered. Since the acceleration constants K = GM and k = Gm, this universal law of gravitational force identifies the mass of any object as the source of the gravitational acceleration of all other objects toward it.

Gravitational field lines

Gravity is an acceleration vector at every point in space - the gravitational acceleration a small mass would experience at that point. According to universal gravitation, to compute this, you have to add the acceleration vectors GM/r² directed toward every other particle of mass M and distance r in the universe. Start this program by considering the acceleration produced by just a single idealized point mass - a particle so much smaller than its distance r that you can ignore its dimensions. The planets are good examples of point masses, since they appear as mere pinpoints of light from anywhere else in the solar system.

A vector associated with every point in space is called a vector field. Faraday invented a way to visualize vector fields about a century after Newton's death. Faraday pictured lines of force filling space - a force field. He was looking at the lines traced by iron filings on a sheet of paper with a magnet beneath, but the picture works for gravity as well. The idea is to connect the vectors into (possibly curved) lines - the path you would follow if you kept moving in the direction the vectors point. So you picture the gravity of a point mass as myriad radial rays converging to the point, like long pins in a tiny pincushion. Each ray is called a field line. Notice that a field line is a directed line - like a one-way street. This field picture captures the direction of the acceleration vector everywhere, but at first glance it seems to lose any indication of magnitude - the length of the arrow.

However, notice that the spacing between the lines decreases as they converge to the point mass. You can measure their density at a given position along some field line by imagining a small loop around the field line. The loop will encircle other nearby lines, and you can define the density of field lines at that location to be the number of field lines threading through this tiny loop, divided by the area of the loop. If you move farther away from the point mass, you need to scale up the diameter of the loop in proportion to the distance for it to encircle a fixed set of field lines, so the loop area scales as the square of the distance from the point mass. Thus, the number of field lines per unit area scales inversely as the square of the distance! If you make the total number of lines emanating from the point proportional to its mass, the magnitude of the gravitational acceleration will be proportional to the number of field lines per unit area!

So far, you assumed the plane of the loop was perpendicular to the field lines. What happens if the plane of the loop is tilted relative to the field lines? The number of lines threading the loop will be proportional to the area of the projection of the loop into the plane perpendicular to the field line. The ratio of that projected area to the loop area when it was oriented normally is exactly the same as the ratio of the component of the acceleration normal to the tilted loop to the full acceleration, which is the component along the field line. Therefore, for any small loop, the component of acceleration normal to the plane of the loop is the number of field lines threading the loop per unit area. More subtly, you need to choose the sign of the component to be negative if the directed field lines thread the loop in the direction opposite to its normal.

Field line geometry

Newton faced an immediate technical problem with this theory of universal gravitation: In the argument that the Earth's gravity diminishes as the square of distance between your downward acceleration on its surface and the acceleration of the Moon in its orbit, you assumed that your "distance from the Earth" was the radius of the Earth, that is, your distance from the center of the Earth. But with universal gravitation, the idea is that each little piece of the Earth - every rock and grain of sand and drop of magma in the whole ball - is individually pulling on you. When you add up all those individual forces in different directions, why should you get the same answer as if all that mass were concentrated at the center of the Earth? Unlike Newton, you can use the field line picture to answer this question.

Imagine a closed surface of any size (eventually this will be the surface of the Earth). A point mass at some arbitrary position produces an acceleration which varies varies as you move around to different points on the surface. The component of that vector normal to the surface (take the inward normal) thus also varies around the surface. The field picture makes it easy to compute the area-weighted average of this normal component of acceleration over the whole surface: Since the normal acceleration component is the number of lines per unit area threading any small loop on the surface, its area-weighted average is just the total number of field lines piercing the surface divided by the total surface area! The one caveat is that you have to count each inward piercing line as plus one, and each outward piercing line as minus one, in order to be consistent about the sign of the acceleration relative to the surface.

There are two cases: First, if the point mass is outside the surface, then each field line pierces the surface twice - once going inward and a second time going outward, so the net number of piercings is zero. Second, if the point mass is inside the surface, then each field line pierces just once, on its way inward. (If the surface is not convex, there may be additional pairs of inward and outward piercings for some rays, but such pairs cancel each other.) Thus, if the point mass is outside the surface, the mean value of the normal component of gravitational acceleration over the whole surface is always zero. When the point mass is inside the surface, on the other hand, the mean value of the normal acceleration is always the total number of field lines emanating from the point mass, divided by the total surface area. The average inward acceleration is proportional to the mass inside the closed surface divided by its surface area.

A second point mass adds its acceleration vector to the acceleration from the first at every point in space. Because the acceleration vectors simply add, the averages of the inward component of the acceleration also simply add. Thus, if the new point is inside the surface, the average increases by the new mass divided by the surface area, otherwise the new mass contributes nothing. Thus, the inverse square law of universal gravitation has the remarkable property that the average value of the inward acceleration over any closed surface is simply proportional the the total mass inside that surface, divided by the surface area.

For any spherically symmetric object, the acceleration at its surface must point directly inward toward the center, and have the same value at every point of the surface - which is therefore the average inward component of the acceleration. Thus, the gravitational acceleration at the surface depends only on the total mass of the object divided by the surface area. How the mass is distributed does not matter, as long as it is spherically symmetric (otherwise the acceleration would differ from place to place around the surface). In particular, the acceleration at the surface is the same as if all the mass were condensed to a single point at its center. You have resolved Newton's technical problem, and universal gravitation has passed an important consistency test.

By construction, any of those field lines is parallel to the gravitational acceleration at any location on the line. Choose the lines to be uniformly distributed in direction. Imagine a series of spherical surfaces centered on the mass point. If you draw a narrow cone, it will intersect any of the spheres in a small circle, and it is clear that the number of rays passing through that circle is the same for all the spheres. Here's the clever part: The area of any circle is proportional to the square of the radius of its sphere, which means that the density of the field lines - the number of lines per unit area on any sphere - falls inversely as the square of the distance from the mass point at its center. Therefore, in this gravitational field picture, the direction of the acceleration is the direction of the field line, and the strength of the field is proportional to the density of field lines - meaning the number per unit area transverse to the field direction. Field lines are a geometrical model of the inversse square law.

The small circles on the spheres are oriented normal to the radial field lines, so the number of field lines per unit area passing through a circle is proportional to the component of the field normal to the circle. If the circle were tilted ninety degrees so its plane was vertical, then no field lines would pass through it - and the horizontal component of the field, that is the component normal to a vertical circle, also is zero. Hence, for both a horizontal and a vertical circle, the number of field lines per unit area passing through the circle is proportional to the component of the field normal to the circle. By projecting a circle oriented at any intermediate tilt angle into a horizontal plane (tangent to one of the spheres), you can see tha the number of field lines passing through the circle will always be proportional to the component of the field normal to the circle.

This picture of field lines makes it particularly easy to understand why any spherical mass distribution produces exactly the same gravitational acceleration outside the sphere as if all the mass were concentrated at the center of the sphere. By the assumed spherical symmetry, you know that the total gravitational acceleration everywhere must be directed toward the center of the sphere.

Consider the gravitational attraction between you on the surface, and some arbitrary speck of matter inside the sphere. Imagine the gravitational field lines emanating from that speck. Remember that the number of field lines per unit area passing through any small patch on the surface of the sphere is proportional to the component of the field normal to the patch, that is, to the component of the field that points straight toward the center. The radial component naturally varies from location to location on the surface, larger the closer the location is to the speck. But the average of the radial component of the field is easy to compute - just add up the total number of field lines passing through any set of small patches covering the whole surface of the Earth and divide by the total area of all the patches. But no matter where the speck is inside the Earth, that is just the total number of field lines divided by the total surface area of the Earth. That is, the average radial component of the gravitational field over the whole surface of the Earth is always exactly the same, no matter where the speck is located inside the Earth!

Do you see it? If you add specks of the same tiny mass anywhere inside the sphere, the average radial component of the gravitational field just increases proportionally to the mass you've added. Furthermore, you can rearrange the positions of the specks however you like within the sphere without changing the average. In particlar, the arrangement with all of the specks crammed into the center will produce exactly the same average radial component of the gravitational acceleration at the surface as any other arrangement of mass inside the sphere. Since any spherically symmetric arrangement of mass produces the same gravitational acceleration at every point on the surface, that common value is clearly the average value, which is the same for any arrangement of mass!

Newton's theory of universal gravitation, the notion that every individual speck of matter attracts every other, passes this first internal consistency check: Because of the inverse square law, a spherically symmetric body - an idealized Earth - does indeed produce the same gravitational acceleration everywhere outside it, as if all its mass were concentrated at its center. The picture of gravitational field lines aligned with the direction of acceleration, with the number of lines per unit area representing the magnitude of the acceleration, gives you some powerful insight into the geometrical nature of the inverse square law.

Obviously it can't be exactly true, because the Earth isn't exactly spherical, so it's not obvious what "center" even means. Instead, you have to ask the question for an idealized perfectly spherically symmetric Earth. The gravitational force or acceleration anywhere outside such an idealized Earth must certainly be directed toward its center: If you divide such an Earth into two hemispheres by any vertical plane (containing you and Earth's center), then the vertical components of the gravitational forces between you and each half must be the same, while the horizontal components normal to the plane must cancel. So the direction of the force is clearly straight downward toward the center, and the only question is if its magnitude is the same as if all the mass were concentrated at the center.

The planets move according to Kepler's laws, which are equivalent to a single inverse square law: The planets are always accelerating directly toward the Sun at a rate inversely proportional to the Sun-planet distance squared. Comets with highly elliptical orbits are accelerating according to the same law, that is, at the same rate as the planets when at the same distance from the Sun. The Galilean moons of Jupiter obey the same law relative to Jupiter, but with a constant of proportionality a thousand times smaller than the planets and comets relative to the Sun. Finally, the acceleration of our Moon is smaller than the acceleration of a falling apple by the square of the ratio of their distances from the center of the Earth. The inverse square acceleration law constant for Earth is roughly three hundred times smaller than for Jupiter.

These are all statements of experimental fact - a distillation of decades, if not millenia, of careful observations of the sky. This massive data reduction required great skill in mathematics, ranging from geometry and arithmetic through calculus - and every single one of those skills was developed, at least in part, to work specifically on the problem of planetary motion.

Separately, Newton formulated three general laws of motion by thinking about the mechanics of earthbound objects. His theory of gravity emerges when you interpret the experimental laws of planetary motion according to these general laws of mechanics. This bold idea - essentially that the Earth and heavenly bodies are just very large rocks - follows directly from the experimental fact that apples and the Moon follow the inverse square acceleration law. In other words, you already know that the gravity you feel on Earth is what holds the Moon in its orbit. What if the pull of Earth extends all the way to the Sun? Indeed, what if the Sun and Jupiter extert their own gravitational pull to hold their planets and moons in orbit? What if every particle of matter pulls on every other?

Isaac Newton, the great mathematician, translated Kepler's laws of planetary motion into an inverse square law of acceleration. From the fact that the Moon, 60 times farther from Earth's center than we are, moves with a downward acceleration 3600 (60²) times less than objects we drop on Earth's surface, Newton the great physicist deduced that the gravity we all feel at the surface of the Earth is exactly what keeps the Moon circling the Earth. Gravity not holds us down, it is binds the whole solar system together.

The reason Newton was thinking about acceleration in the first place was to apply his general theory of mechanics - how things move - to the planets. When he did, he discovered a universal law of gravity. Universal gravitation explains why the Moon does not accurately orbit according to Kepler's laws, how the Moon and Sun produce ocean tides, the tiny deviations in in planetary orbits from Kepler's laws, and many other puzzles.

Here you will follow Newton's physical reasoning about mechanics and planetary motion. Although Newton's model of gravity remains the easiest way to think about both solar system dynamics and motion in general, and despite its overwhelming success in describing a wide variety of phenomena, Einstein pointed out a different way to picture gravity. Just as Newton's model of gravity began as a different, but equivalent, way to think about Kepler's laws of planetary motion, so Einstein's model of gravity begins as a different, but equivalent, way to think about Newton's inverse square force law. All three pictures remain relevant today - astronomers choose the viewpoint best suited to describe the problem at hand.

Mass and weight

The translated Kepler's laws state that any bodies at the same distance from an attracting center will share a common acceleration, no matter their size: A cannonball and a bullet accelerate downward at the same rate, and a comet is accelerating toward the Sun at the same rate as Earth when it crosses Earth's orbit. That means the gravitational force must be directly proportional to the mass of the falling body, according to Newton's second law of motion.

However, if gravity pulls you toward the Earth, then Newton's third law says you must pull up on the Earth with an equal force - the force down on you is the action, and the force up on the Earth is the equal and opposite reaction. Similarly, if the gravity of the Sun holds the Earth in orbit, the Earth must pull back with the same force on the Sun. The second law told you the Sun's gravitational force on the Earth is proportional to Earth's mass, since you know that any sized object at the same distance would share the same acceleration. Equal and opposite reaction

Newton knew from Kepler that the planets accelerate directly toward the Sun at a rate inversely proportional to the square of their distance. Thus, since F = ma, the Sun must pull on the planet with a force proportional to the mass of the planet (and inversely proportional to the square of the distance). But you also know that each planet (or both Jupiter and Earth, at least) causes things to accelerate toward it according to an inverse square distance law. If this plantary inverse square law extends all the way to the Sun, then the planet must be causing the Sun to accelerate (very slightly) toward it as well - which means the planet pulls on the Sun with a force proportional to the solar mass.

But the force of the Sun pulling on the planet must equal the force of the planet pulling on the Sun, since actions must have equal and opposite reactions. (The forces are clearly in opposite directions along the Sun-planet line.) Since the pull of the Sun is proportional to the planet's mass and the pull of the planet proportional to the Sun's mass, the Sun-planet force must be directly proportional to both masses, as well as inversely proportional to the square of the distance. From this, Newton proposed his law of universal gravitation - that a force acts between any pair of bodies directly proportional to both masses, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, F = GMm/r².

Notice that the inverse square law acceleration constant for a body of mass M is GM, directly proportional to its mass. Thus, Newton deduced that the Sun is roughly a thousand times the mass of Jupiter, while Jupiter is a bit over three hundred times the mass of Earth, since those are the ratios of their acceleration constants from Kepler's laws. This is still the only way we know the masses of stars, planets, asteroids, or black holes - by measuring the accelerations of nearby objects.

On the one hand, universal gravitation represents a huge simplification - a single inverse square force law explains all manner of phenomena, from the motion of the planets or cannonballs to the tides. On the other hand, you realize that in order to compute the motion of any object, you need to account for the forces between every rock and drop of fluid in the whole universe! Every pair of rocks attract each other, so actually applying the "simple" law can become very complicated indeed.
