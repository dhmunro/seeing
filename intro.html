Seeing and Touching the Solar System

How Kepler and Newton revolutionized science

Isaac Newton famously said that he stood on the shoulders of giants. Johannes Kepler was surely one of those giants.

Newton wrote the book that is still the cornerstone of modern physics and engineering. Published in 1687, his Principia immediately generated an electric thrill and shock not only among the scientific community, but also among the public at large. The most mind-blowing new idea is that the force of gravity we have always known on earth extends into the heavens, governing the motion of the moon and planets just as it governs the motion of a thrown stone.

Who provided Newton with the detailed and accurate description of how the planets in fact move? In 1609, Kepler published his first two laws of planetary motion, finally providing the definitive answer the the ancient question of how the planets move. A decade later, in 1620, having worked out the orbital parameters for the remaining planets, Kepler published his third law, the quantitative relationship between a planet's distance from the sun and its period. Although these summary laws are still remembered and used in astronomy, Kepler's more ingenious and original contribution was the technique he invented to survey the orbits of Earth and Mars, which is largely untaught and unknown today.

Seeing the Solar System is the story of Kepler's laws of planetary motion, beginning with his brilliantly original surveying technique, then showing how he used his survey results to discover his laws.

Touching the Solar System is the story of Newton's law of universal gravity, beginning with how he recast Kepler's laws into the language of rates of change, then showing how he wove planetary motion into his more general laws of motion.

The aim of these two webpages is to present what Kepler and Newton did using animated drawings, without any algebra. In the case of Newton, this is not quite possible - you will need some high school geometry and relatively simple algebraic ideas. However, there will be very few gaps requiring additional research or work to fill: You can feel the full weight of the logic yourself, and hopefully gain some understanding of how a modern scientist thinks. Think about when logic can serve as the basis for authority and belief. Think about the indispensible role of models for interpreting everything you see and experience: Theory is a necessity, not a luxury.
