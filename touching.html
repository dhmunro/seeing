Touching the Solar System

How Newton recast Kepler's Laws and grounded the heavens

Isaac Newton discovered laws of motion which not only apply to ordinary things on Earth, but also govern the planets and moons in our solar system. His analysis set the standard for all future generations of physicists; our mantra ever since has been, "Think like Newton."

The natural way to study motion is to begin with trajectory - the arc of a cannonball or the orbit of a planet. You then come up with a rule for how the object advances along this trajectory in time. Galileo discovered that the canonball moves in a parabola with a constant horizontal velocity, and this is exactly the pattern of Kepler's first two laws: The planet moves in an ellipse with the Sun at one focus, such that the line between the Sun and planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

Newton demoted the trajectory into a secondary feature of any motion, promoting instantaneous position, velocity, and acceleration to be the primary features. When a rigid body is free to move, Newton recognized that its velocity remains fixed when no forces act on it. Pushing on it changes its velocity directly, only indirectly changing their position. That is, force causes acceleration, the rate of change of velocity.

Newton was not only the greatest physicist of his age, but also its greatest mathematician. He invented a new mathematical tool - the infinitessimal calculus - specifically to be able to work with motion in this radically new way. With calculus, Newton could derive the trajectories and rules of movement from his more fundamental instantaneous positions, velocities, and accelerations. In the jargon of calculus, velocity and acceleration are the derivatives - instantaneous rates of change - of position and velocity, and you integrate them to find the trajectory - how they evolve over finite times.

So what are the velocity and acceleration of a planet according to Kepler's laws?

Kepler's Second Law

The radius vector from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times as the planet moves. Thus, Kepler's second law is already a statement about a rate of change: The area per unit time a planet's radius sweeps out is some fixed constant.

[Picture shows ellipse with fixed time/area sector drawn.]

Newton's first law states that absent any forces, a body will move in a straight line at a constant speed. Then the radius vector from an arbitrary fixed point to the body will sweep out equal triangular areas in equal times: The area is half the height (distance from the fixed point to the line) times the base. The base is the same for equal times since the body moves at constant speed. The rate area is swept out is just half the height times the speed.

[Picture changes to straight line motion under point with fixed time/area triangle drawn.]

The radius and velocity vectors are Newton's preferred way to describe motion. Notice that if you decompose the velocity vector into components in the radial and transverse directions, then the right triangle with the radius as its hypotenuse and height as one side is similar to the triangle with the whole velocity as its hypotenuse and the transverse velocity component as one side. Thus the rate of change of area is also half the product of the radius and the transverse component of velocity.

[Picture changes to show similar position and velocity triangles, then the two equal area hs = rv triangles.]

If the velocity suddenly changes - so quickly that the change in position is negligible - then the rate of change of area will remain unchanged if and only if the transverse component of velocity remains unchanged.

[Picture changes to show a deflecting trajectory, with the equal area triangles before and after the deflection. Add more and closer spaced radial deflections until trajectory becomes smooth curve.]

When you say that a planet moves around the Sun, what you are really saying is that its velocity is continually being deflected towards the Sun. You just reasoned that if the rate the radius vector sweeps out area remains constant, then only the radial component of velocity can be changing. (Although the transverse velocity component does not change over infinitessimal times, over finite times it does change because the radial and transverse directions are changing!) That is, Kepler's second law means that a planet's acceleration is directly toward the Sun.

[Picture changes to show the original elliptical trajectory with the moving shaded sector, then adding the velocity vector and inward radial acceleration vector, finally fading out the shaded sector and trajectory for just the velocity and radial acceleration vectors.]

So the planets are accelerating directly toward the Sun. Newton realized that "sweeping out equal areas in equal times" is just a rather clumsy and roundabout way to say that the planets are being pulled straight toward the Sun.

Three ways to define an ellipse

Kepler's first law is much harder to recast in Newtonian terms. You need to know many properties of an ellipse and its foci to even get started. Precisely what is an ellipse and what are its foci? In simplest terms, an ellipse is a circle elongated along one axis. More precisely, an ellipse is the shape of a section of a right circular cylinder cut by a plane not normal to its axis - think of slicing a cucumber on a bias.

[Picture of circle stretching into ellipse, then add 3D cylinder with slicing plane and rotate in 3D to an orientation you might see if you were slicing a cucumber.]

To find one focus of this ellipse, introduce a sphere of the same radius as the cylinder and slide its center along the axis of the cylinder until it just touches the cutting plane. That tangency point is one focus of the ellipse.

[Picture shows sphere moving down axis, stopping when it touches the cutting plane, then rotating to show face on view ellipse with focus, then back to side on view of cylinder.]

Let point S be the focus, point C be the center of the ellipse (where the cylinder axis intersects the cutting plane), point B be the point on the ellipse farthest from C on the same side as S, and point A be on the cylinder such that CAB is a right angle whose plane is normal to the cutting plane. CA is the radius of the sphere and cylinder, or half the short axis of the ellipse, CB is half the long axis of the ellipse, and BA equals CS, the distance of the focus from the center of the ellipse. The ratio CS/CB (or BA/CB) is the eccentricity, or ε, of the ellipse. As ε varies from 0 to 1, the ellipse varies from a circle to an infinitely elongated cigar.

[Picture labels S, rolls to top view of slice, then triangle CAB fades in. A copy of this triangle rotates and flips to show CS=AB - perhaps rolling a bit to turn the flip into a continuous rotation about CA.]

Choose any point on the ellipse P. SP is tangent to the sphere at S. Draw DP along the surface of the cylinder parallel to its axis, with D on the circle where the sphere touches the cylinder. Then DP has the same length as SP, because all tangents to a sphere drawn from a point outside the sphere have the same length. Slide a copy of the tangent circle down the cylinder until the focus S lies in the plane of this new circle, and extend DP to reach the new circle at E. Draw right triangle GED with G in the cutting plane and GED perpendicular to the cutting plane. Note that triangle GEP is always the same shape as CAB - only its size changes as P moves around the ellipse. Therefore PE/GP equals the eccentricity ε. In other words, PE equals εGP - a fixed fraction of GP.

[Picture mutates to show construction of DP, highlighting SP and DP in the same color, and letting P orbit the ellipse once to show SP=DP. Show second circle moving along cylinder, and intersection line of its plane with cutting plane, stopping when that intersection line reaches S. Add point G on that line and extend DP to E to form right triangle GED. Again, let P orbit once showing constancy of DE. Finally, roll cylinder to top view to show GEP is same shape as CAB.]

Now the length of DE is just the distance between the two circles, no matter what P you choose. Since DP equals SP, the sum SP+PE must remain constant (equal to DE) as P moves around the ellipse. Note that you need to regard PE as an algebraic quantity with a sign, positive when P is between D and E, but negative when E is between D and P. This relationship provides a second way to define the ellipse: The locus of all points P such that SP+εGP equals a constant. If you write εGP for PE here, then unlike the sliced cylinder definition of an ellipse, this definition doesn't involve the third dimension.

[Picture rolls away from top view of slice, then shows how GEP changes as P orbits the ellipse, and how the sign of PE changes. Rotate to view ellipse plane. Finally, let P orbit ellipse to show how SP+εGP remains constant?]

You can slide a second sphere into the opposite end of the cylinder, defining a second focus, call it F. As you did for the first focus, connect an arbitrary point P on the ellipse to point H on the circle where the second sphere touches the cylinder. Now FP equals HP, just as before you found SP and DP equal. But DP+PH=DH, which is always the distance between the centers of the two spheres. DH equals the long axis of the ellipse, twice CB. Thus the ellipse is the set of points P for which SP+FP=DH, that is, for which the sum of the distances from its two foci is a fixed constant equal to the long axis of the ellipse. Again, this is a purely two dimensional definition.

[Picture rotates back to 3D view. Second sphere appears and slides to second focus. Circle plane containng S slides to position where second sphere touches cylinder. HP and FP appear in same color, contrasting with SP and DP to show equal length pairs. P orbits ellipse once to show DH stays constant length. Roll to top view to show DH equals long axis of ellipse.]

In the plane of the ellipse, you can extend SP to a point Q, where PQ equals FP. As P moves around the ellipse, Q moves around a circle, since SQ=SP+PF remains constant. Relative to point F, SQ is an eccentric circle. Its eccentricity - offset of its center S from F as a fraction of its radius - is FS/SQ. Notice that FS/SQ equals ε, the same as the eccentricity of the ellipse.

[Picture rotates back to ellipse plane, then copy of FP rotates around P to Q. P orbits the ellipse, tracing the circle SQ. P pauses when FP vertical to show circle and ellipse have same eccentricity.]

Relation between eccentric circle and ellipse

You also need to know how to construct the tangent to an ellipse. (A planet's velocity is tangent to its ellipse!) Draw the midpoint M of FQ, and consider a point N on the line MP, the perpendicular bisector of FQ. When N is at P, it is on the ellipse, so that SN+NF=DH. At any other point, SN+NF=SN+NQ must be larger, since SPQ is a straight line. Thus, every other point N on MP is outside of the ellipse, so that MP must be tangent to the ellipse at P, since it intersects the ellipse only at the one point P.

[Picture adds M and line through MP, then N on MP and bent line SNQ, as well as FN. N slides back and forth over P showing that FN+SN always greater than FP+SP, so N outside ellipse.]

Extend QS to I such that FIQ is a right angle, making IQ the projection of FQ onto SQ. Notice that the ratio IS/SQ is the product of IS/FS and ε=FS/SQ. Triangles SPG and FSI are the same shape, so that IS/FS equals GP/SP, and the ratio IS/SQ equals εGP/SP. Remember that SP+εGP is the same for every point P, which means that SP times (1+εGP/SP) is constant, independent of P. But the length of IQ is SQ+IS, which is the constant length SQ times (1+εGP/SP). Since IQ varies in direct proportion to (1+εGP/SP) as P moves around the ellipse, and SP varies inversely as this same quantity, the product of SP and IQ remains unchanged as P moves.

[Picture removes N, fades M and line MP. Adds I and highlights SPG and FSI, highlighting IS/FS and GP/SP. No obvious way to picture the remainder of this argument, but should probably have P orbit the ellipse while highlighting IS+SQ and EP+SP to indicate they are split in the same ratio.]

Now rotate FIQ ninety degrees around S. FQ is now parallel to the ellipse tangent at P, so PJ, a translated copy of FQ, will be tangent to the ellipse at P. Let SK be a second translated copy of FQ. Then SPJK is a parallelogram with base SP and height IQ, so its area is the invariant product SP times IQ. Therefore the area of SPJK does not change as P moves around the ellipse! Notice that you can apply an arbitrary scale factor to triangle FIQ without changing the fact that the area of SPJK is independent of P.

[Picture rotates FIQ, shows the copies of FQ translating to P and S - the copy that translates to S also translates a copy of IQ, to show that IQ is the height of SPJK. Highlight area, and let P move once around the ellipse. Lastly, scale FIQ to smaller size and orbit again.]

Kepler's First Law

If FQ (or PJ) were the velocity vector of the planet at P, then the rate of change of area in the instantaneous version of Kepler's Second Law would be the area of triangle SPJ, which is just half the constant area SPJK. Hence, not only is FQ tangent to the ellipse at P, but its length changes in exactly the right way to satisfy the equal areas in equal times law. Thus, you've demonstrated that the velocity vector of a planet P traces an eccentric circle with eccentricity equal to the eccentricity of its ellipse, but rotated ninety degrees!

[Picture separates FSQ and velocity circle from SP and ellipse, then adds a small copy of FSQ with F at P. SP and all three sides of FSQ are now drawn as vector arrows. Letter labels fade. P orbits ellipse a couple of times. Then eccentricity changes for another couple of orbits, after which eccentricity returns to original.]

To complete Newton's program, you need only find the force law, that is, the acceleration. Since the radius of the velocity circle is unchanging with time, the velocity change is always tangent to that circle, ninety degrees ahead of SQ, which itself has been rotated to be ninety degrees ahead of SP. Thus the direction of the acceleration is always exactly opposite SP, that is, directly toward the Sun, as you had already deduced.

[Picture adds acceleration arrow to tip of velocity on velocity diagram, and possibly in the position diagram as well in a common color to show how it remains exactly opposite the radius vector SP.]

As for the magnitude of the acceleration, it is the instantaneous angular speed (in radians per unit time) of Q around the velocity circle, times the radius SQ of the velocity circle. Since SQ is always ninety degress from SP, the angular speed of Q around S is identical to the angular speed of P around S. But the angular speed of P around S times the distance SP is the transverse velocity of P, and the transverse velocity times SP is twice the rate SP sweeps out area. Hence the rate area is being swept out, which is a constant, is (half) the angular speed times the square of SP. In other words, the instantaneous angular speed of a planet varies inversely as the square of SP. Since acceleration is the constant SQ times this angular speed, you have the force law:

[Indicate angular speed in both diagrams, with P orbiting ellipse.]

The acceleration of a planet is always directly toward the Sun with magnitude inversely proportional to the square of its distance from the Sun. If the acceleration of a planet obeys this inverse square law, then it will move around an ellipse with the Sun at one focus according to the equal area law. Its velocity vector will move around an eccentric circle with the same eccentricity as its ellipse, but rotated ninety degrees. You have completed Newton's program, describing orbits in terms of their instantaneous position and velocity, plus a force law.

Kepler's Third Law

So far, all you know is that each planet accelerates toward the Sun at a rate inversely proportional to the square of its distance. The constant of proportionality in this inverse square law might still be different from one planet to the next. But notice that the dimensions of this constant of proportionality are acceleration times length squared, or length cubed divided by time squared - exactly the same dimensions as the constant of proportionality in Kepler's cube-square law. Does a single inverse square law explain the accelerations of all the planets?

Since the ellipse is just a circle elongated on one axis, its area is A=𝝅ab (the stretched 𝝅r²), where a and b are its long and short semi-axes (that is, measured from the center like the radius of a circle). If T is the period of the planet, then the constant area swept out per unit time is A/T, and you know the constant of proportionality in the inverse square law for the angular speed is just twice this, or 2𝝅ab/T. If v is the constant radius of the eccentric velocity circle, then the constant of proportionality in the inverse square law for the acceleration is v times 2𝝅ab/T.

[Show area change by stretching rectangle with inscribed circle?]

But twice the area rate 2𝝅ab/T is also the transverse velocity times the planet radius SP. Choose the instant when SP is perpendicular to the long axis of the ellipse because at that moment the transverse velocity equals the constant radius of the eccentric velocity circle, call it v. Going all the way back to the diagrams you drew of the ellipse as a cylinder slice, at this moment the planet radius SP is b²/a. Hence, vb²/a = 2𝝅ab/T, or v = 2𝝅a²/(Tb).

[Picture shows this position of SP and v, rotate in 3D ending at top view of slice to show DE is b2/a.]

Putting these two formulas together, the constant of proportionality in the inverse square law for acceleration is 4𝝅²a³/T². But Kepler's Third Law is that a³/T² is the same for every planet, so the constant of proportionality in Newton's inverse square law for the acceleration is the same for every planet!

Thus, all three of Kepler's laws follow from the single statement that a planet accelerates directly toward the Sun at a rate inversely proportional to the square of its distance from the Sun.
